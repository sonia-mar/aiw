Worker's Testimonals

Álvaro Ramírez

Mi nombre es Álvaro Ramírez. Vengo de Santo Domingo. Y trabajé, primero cuando llegué es en el ‘90. Trabajé una factoría, duré 3 años en la factoría. Salí por falta-- no pagaban bien. No pagaban bien, salí, hice el curso de home attendant. Duré dos meses, hice cursos, y busqué trabajo en la agencia, First Chinese. Allí empecé trabajando 24 horas. De las 24 horas acaso pagaban 13. No pagaban las 24 completas. Pasando mucho en la casa del cliente, en la calle, aguantando muchas cosas tanto del paciente como de la familia del paciente. Dicen que se duerme pero no se duerme, mentira. Allí no… 24 no es para dormir no se duerme, porque hay que estar pendiente al paciente, y estar con su medicina y de diferentes horas, y en realidad no se duerme. Entonces estamos tratando de ver ya eso, ese dinero perdido estamos tratando de recuperar. Los chavos que estamos perdidos, las horas que nos quitaron, que nos robaron. De 24 horas, pagando 13. Es mucha la que se quedan con ella. No pagan. Entonces estamos luchando y tratando, aunque no, por ejemplo, me pagan a mí, si otros resuelven, puede uno se conforme.

Jian Hua Deng

I’ve been a care worker for 6 years, working 24-hour shifts from 2012 to 2014. I worked 3 to 5 days a week. My patient Grandma Sun had colon cancer, so the post-surgery care work had to be meticulous. We also had to take care of her husband. At night the two of them would get out of bed at different times, and I had to get up with them to take care of them. Usually at night they would get up 5 or 6 times, so I had no way of continuously sleeping more than 2 hours a night. I joined a lawsuit to ask for 24 hours of wages. The judges have already made a decision on some of these cases, that 24-hour workers should get 24 hours of wages. But the boss does not act according to the law, and the Department of Labor not only doesn’t help workers, but actually uses emergency regulation to suppress our demands! We went to the union to complain, but to no avail. How is this leaving us women workers with any lifelines?! The system of 24-hour shifts shouldnn't continue! For the health and vitality of workers and patients, splitting into 2 shifts is very necessary. This is the voice of home attendants.

Hui Ling Chen

I take care of a patient that needs 24-hour care. She pees and poops on her bed. Sometimes the patient can’t poop it out and thus is in pain. I put on plastic gloves to scoop the stool out. Every night, I had to turn over her body 3 times, to lessen her pain. In order to make a living, I’ve neglected my own family to do 24- hour home attendance work. The company told home attendants do not give water to patients at night, do not get up from bed to change their diapers, ignore the patient when they call for help. We can’t do something this inhumane. Eliminate the 24-hour work day system! And compensate all the hours we worked as 24-hrs home attendants. This is our appeal as home attendants!

Rui Ling Wang

I’ve been a 24-hour care worker for 10 whole years. My patient’s skin was severely scarred after a burn injury, and she had just come back from the hospital. Her ankles had grown ulcers, rotten and torn, and her buttocks were also burned rotten. We home attendants would everyday take care of the patient’s entire body, flipping her over, wiping her down, changing her clothes, washing her hair, changing her diapers and so on. When the weather was nice we would use a hoisting frame to transfer the patient to a wheelchair to go outside and bathe in the sun. At night, the patient would flip around and change diapers, calling out for me at any given moment, at least 3 to 4 times. There was no possible way to get more than 5 hours of continuous sleep. Long-term 24-hour care work made me anxious, nervous, sleep-deprived, with aching limbs and waist, high blood pressure, and stomach disease. You could say that I too become a “patient”. Also, the building where the patient lived hosted a lot of gamblers. Oftentimes at midnight the police would knock on the door to investigate, creating yet another form of pressure and worry. I demand that the government stop the system of 24-hour workdays. Splitting this into 2 shifts is better for the patient and the worker.

Xiu Qiong Zhong

The patient I took care of was 90 years old, who had very little energy. Walking and going to the toilet, I had to use a lot of energy to support and pull her up, so that she wouldn’t fall down. When night came she would not even sleep, calling for me very often, and wouldn’t let me go to sleep either. If she peed in her clothes, I would also need to help her change. So it was hard, strenuous work. I worked 24 hours a day, head hurting and dizzy the entire day. My shoulders and hands also hurt unbearably. Because my hands hurt, I asked the agency for leave of absence for a period of time. The agency said that if I stopped, my work stopped. Because the company was not giving me work, my home attendant license was discontinued, and to this day I still haven’t found any work. I must rely on my child to live and support me.

Lai Yee Chan

Lai Yee Chan, a home attendant for the CPC (Chinese-American Planning Council), helped her patients day and night for over 15 years. During the day, she bathed and fed her patients. At night, she helped them get out of bed and go to the bathroom or get a drink of water. She could not leave her patient for even a second of her 24-hour shift. One day her daughter was locked out of her house, but Lai Yee was not allowed to go downstairs to give her daughter the key. Adding insult to injury, Lai Yee has not seen a pay increase in 8 years, is not paid for the 12 hours of work at night, except for a small fee, and is never paid overtime. Ultimately, workers like Lai Yee are paid about $5/hour, way less than the minimum wage.
